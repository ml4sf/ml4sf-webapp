Machine learning for singlet fission

At а time when petrol, coal and fossil gas are depleting and our air, water and soil are irreparably polluted, we need more than ever green alternatives of energy production. Recently, it has been demonstrated that а successful strategy to improve the productivity of solar cells is the use of materials that undergo singlet fission (SF). In the conventional photovoltaics one absorbed photon invokes electronic excitation and creates 2 charge carriers – one positive and one negative. In singlet fission, an excited molecule is able to 'share' the excitation with an adjacent one and thus one photon creates 4 charge carriers – 2 positive and 2 negative. Accordingly, the efficiency of the solar cells may be doubled by incorporation of a singlet fission material.

It might seem that the singlet fission chromophores are the Holy Grail of photovoltaics; however, such molecules are hard to find because, at the molecular level, they should satisfy a series of demanding requirements from thermodynamics and photophysical point of view. Therefore, the quest for new singlet fission materials is a very challenging task and resembles the search of a needle in a haystack.

Our Project aims at performing large scale computational screening for new SF materials. For this purpose, we combined quantum-chemical and machine learning approaches. We have developed an artificial neural network model that predicts potential singlet fission chromophores based on their diradical character. The use of the diradical character as a preselection tool for singlet fission propensity is based on the recent works of Nakano and co-workers, which reveal that molecules with diradical character between 0.1 and 0.5 are promising singlet fission materials (https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/jz2015346). This web application allows you to join our hunting adventure for new singlet fission chromophores and to use our model with your own molecule!

Step by step guide for use

Get inspired

The first step is to prepare the molecular structure that you want to test for potential singlet fission propensity. You can extract a molecule from the open-access PubChem database (or any other database) or design your own molecule.

Prepare the input

Our web application requires as an input a molecular structure data in .mol format. Currently, we can support structures with up to XXXXXX heavy (non-hydrogen) atoms. There are several options you can use to prepare the mol file. To draw the structure, you can use:

  • your favorite molecular modeling software
  • or https://molview.org/

Next step is to save the structure as a mol file.

Run

Once the mol file has been prepared, you have to upload it and press the button “Process“. This will start background calculations and in few seconds/minutes the results will be displayed.

You can run only one molecule at a time.

Get the results

If your molecule is classified by the artificial network model as having zero diradical character, then most probably it is not suitable for singlet fission. If your molecule is classified with non-zero diradical character, then there is high probability that it is suitable for singlet fission.

Please, note that the diradical character is not the only parameter that governs the single fission propensity of a molecule and is used here as a preselection tool. Therefore, once you find a potential chromophore with our model we highly recommend you to perform additional quantum-chemical calculations in order to explore its real singlet fission potential.
